Prepared by: Carlos Camelo and Valentina Yepes

Report Context: Media Performance, Unit Economics, and Growth Drivers

This report provides a consolidated view of media performance across active campaigns, focusing on delivery execution, reach generation, and unit economics to support high-level strategic decision-making.

Impressions and mileage data are sourced directly from the Data team, using internal dashboards and standardized aggregation criteria to ensure consistency and comparability across campaigns and time periods.

Unit economics (e.g., cost per 1,000 miles and CPM) are calculated based on driver-related costs only and are benchmarked against prevailing Mobile OOH market ranges in the U.S. This approach provides a conservative, execution-based view of efficiency, grounded in actual delivered mileage rather than modeled traffic estimates.

The objective is to clearly separate execution performance from market dynamics, enabling informed discussions around optimization levers, pricing strategy, and scalability heading into 2026.

4. Campaign Findings (High-Level)

This section summarizes the most relevant findings by campaign, focused on financial outcomes, operational compliance, and contractual risk areas.

robot.com — Execution & Cost Control

  • Total spend: $19,350 USD ($18,900 launched + $450 cancelled).
  • Savings generated from cancellations: Original commitment was built around ~55 drivers, but we ultimately kept 41 drivers who consistently reported mileage and campaign activity. This ensured reporting quality and reduced unnecessary burn.
  • Financial impact: Potential cost at full commitment was $24,750. Actual cost was $19,350, resulting in $5,400 USD in savings.
  • Primary drivers of savings:
    • Drivers who never installed the wrap were cancelled and removed from billing.
    • Early cancellations (in some cases, only 1 month paid) reduced total campaign liability.
  • High-level takeaway: Strong cost discipline through cancellations improved unit economics and reduced risk, while maintaining a core set of drivers with reliable reporting.

Ponce Law — Compliance, Contract Risk & Evidence

  • Driver credits / mileage delivery: We currently have credit for 5 drivers who met mileage targets. For December, we have 4 credited today, with expectation to return to 5 once the final driver is confirmed.
  • Background checks: Most drivers show background-check infractions; however, only one driver has recent findings that could justify contract termination.
  • Contract governance gap: All drivers signed an older contract version (2022–2023). During renewals, no updated contracts were executed. One contract even shows a signature date of 1958, indicating poor controls and lack of review by the previous team.
  • Contract coverage: At a high level, the legacy agreement is similar to the current version, but it is missing key clauses that provide stronger protections. This is pending legal / operational review.
  • Proof of installation: We found photos for only 4 vehicles with the wrap installed. The remaining evidence is either missing or buried in prior email threads. We will reconfirm with drivers and request updated photos; expectation is that wraps remain installed.

Executive Summary — Cross-Campaign Findings

  • Cost efficiency: robot.com benefited from active cancellation management, generating $5.4K USD savings vs. original commitment, while protecting data quality by retaining only reliable drivers.
  • Operational risk concentration: Ponce Law’s primary risk is governance and compliance (legacy contracts, incomplete documentation, and background-check follow-up) rather than immediate delivery performance.
  • Immediate priorities: (1) complete photo evidence collection, (2) standardize contract versioning and renewals, and (3) tighten cancellation/termination pathways tied to compliance and reporting performance.

5. Operational Process Findings (Prior Administration)

High-level diagnosis of core operational processes under the prior administration. The review highlights structural gaps that impacted efficiency, risk control, and driver experience.

Process Area Key Findings / Concerns
Driver Outreach No defined driver profile or sourcing criteria; outreach was opportunistic and inconsistent, leading to low-quality intake and early driver disengagement.
Negotiation Unstructured negotiations with unclear terms; background checks often occurred after commercial discussions, increasing operational and security risk.
Driver Contracting Disorganized follow-up and legacy contract usage; contracts were signed without standardized review or campaign-specific protections.
Wrap Installation Inconsistent quality, poor coordination, and inconvenient installation logistics resulted in delays, rework, and driver dissatisfaction.
Campaign Launch & Support Lack of clear driver guidelines and unreliable communication channels; recurring issues with the campaign app created friction and brand risk.
Payments & Reimbursements Inconsistent payment timing and low transparency around gas, tolls, and referrals, eroding driver trust and satisfaction.

Overall concern: Processes evolved reactively rather than by design, with limited standardization, weak controls, and heavy manual dependency across the driver lifecycle.

Next step: Rebuild the entire operational area end-to-end, defining clear ownership, standardized processes, and automation. A structured 2026 roadmap will be developed to address these gaps and support scalable, compliant growth.
